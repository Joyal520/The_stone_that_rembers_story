CHAPTER 10

What the Desert Keeps

Years have different names here.

Vihaan is an old man now. He lives in a small house near the edge of a different desert—one that has roads, maps, and cell towers. One that is safe.

Or so people think.

He sits on his porch every evening, watching the sun dip below the horizon. The light turns gold, then red, then purple. It is beautiful. But Vihaan always watches until the very last moment, until the first star appears.

He is waiting for a flicker.

It never comes. Not here. Not yet.

Sometimes, travellers stop by. They ask him why he lives so far from the city. They ask him about the scars on his hands.

He tells them stories.

He tells them about a road that swallowed time. He tells them about a stone that held the world in balance. He tells them about spirits that look like shadows and a city that appears only when the world is broken.

“Is it true?” they ask, their eyes wide.

Vihaan smiles, a slow, dry smile. “It’s just a dream,” he says. “An old man’s dream.”

But every morning, before he eats, he walks to a small rock in his garden. He takes a sharp piece of metal from his pocket and carves a single line into the stone.

One mark for every day the sun rises. One mark for every day the road goes forward.

People think he is counting the days he has left.

He isn’t.

He is keeping the balance. He is remembering.

“Intent matters,” he whispers to the stone.

One afternoon, a jeep drives past his house. It is old, dusty, and rattling with noise. A young man sits behind the wheel, his arm hanging out the window, music playing loud.

He stops to ask for directions.

“I’m looking for the shortcut,” the young man says. “The old road across the salt flats. My map says it saves three hours.”

Vihaan looks at the jeep. He looks at the spare tyre on the back. He looks at the jack handle secured with rusty clamps.

He looks at the young man’s eyes—bright, impatient, fearless.

“There is no shortcut,” Vihaan says quietly.

The young man laughs. “There’s always a shortcut, uncle. You just have to know where to look.”

He revs the engine and drives off, dust billowing in his wake. He heads toward the horizon, toward the place where the map ends and the empty quarter begins.

Vihaan watches him go.

He does not shout. He does not run after him. He knows he cannot stop what has already begun.

The desert is waiting. It has been waiting for a long time.

But this time, Vihaan is not afraid.

He looks down at his hands. The white scars glow faintly in the evening light—not with magic, but with memory.

“It is his turn now,” Vihaan says.

He picks up his metal tool and turns back to his stone. He carves another line, deep and straight.

The sun sets.

The stars come out.

And somewhere, far away, a stone begins to lean.

Do you remember?
