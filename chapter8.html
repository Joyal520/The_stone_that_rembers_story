CHAPTER 8

The Moment of Correction

The reset did not bring silence this time. It brought violence.

Vihaan hit the ground hard, gasping as the air was punched from his lungs. The jeep stood beside him,engine ticking heat. The desert stretched out in all directions—familiar, dark, and empty.

But the stillness was gone.

The wind howled, sounding like a thousand voices screaming at once. Be sand whirled around his feet, stinging his skin. The sky churned, no longer empty, but filled with shadows that twisted and dived like hunting birds.

And in front of him, the stone leaned deeper than ever before. It groaned—a low, grinding sound of rock under immense pressure.

“No time,” Vihaan whispered. He scrambled to his feet.

The spirits were already rising from the sand. They didn’t wait in the distance anymore. They were here, circling him, their forms flickering between shadow and bone.

“YOU CANNOT FIX IT.”

The voices tore at his mind, louder than the wind.

“IT IS BROKEN. YOU ARE BROKEN.”

Vihaan ignored them. He grabbed the long metal jack handle from the back of the jeep—the same tool he had tried to use as a weapon before. Now, it had a different purpose.

He ran to the base of the stone. The ground beneath it was cracked, the sand loose where the impact had shifted the foundation.

He jammed the metal bar under the leaning edge of the monolith. It grated against the rock. He shoved it deeper, finding purchase against a buried slab of bedrock.

“Move,” he gritted out.

He threw his weight against the bar. The stone didn’t budge.

A cold hand brushed the back of his neck. Vihaan flinched but didn’t let go.

“Break yourself,”

a spirit hissed beside his ear. Its face was a void. “Give up. Stay with us.”

Vihaan squeezed his eyes shut. He thought of the old man. He thought of the city. He thought of the feeling of peace he had found there, however brief.

“I am not staying,” he shouted.

He pushed harder. His muscles screamed. The metal bar bent slightly under the strain.

For a moment, nothing happened. Then, he felt it.

A shift. A tiny, almost imperceptible movement.

But as the stone moved, something else happened. A sharp pain spiked in his head—not like a headache, but like something being cut away.

A memory slipped. What was his mother’s face? He reached for it, but it was gone, replaced by gray static.

“The price,” he gasped.

He pushed again. The stone groaned louder.

Another pain. Another loss. The name of his first school—gone. The sound of rain on a roof—gone.

“Stop!” the spirits shrieked. They swarmed him now, clawing at his arms, his back. Their touch burned like ice. Blood soaked his shirt.

Vihaan didn’t stop. He couldn’t. If he let go now, the stone would fall, and the loop would close forever.

He looked down at the base of the stone. A faint glow appeared beneath the crack. The symbols began to shine—not with angry red light, but with a deep, steady gold. The same light he had seen in the city.

“Intent matters,” he whispered to himself.

He wasn’t trying to escape. He wasn’t trying to run away. He was trying to restore.

He focused on that feeling. The desire to fix what he had broken. To set things right, no matter the cost.

With a final, desperate scream, he threw his entire body weight against the lever.

CRACK.

The sound was like a gunshot.

The stone lifted. It shuddered, balanced on the edge for a heartbeat—and then snapped back into place.

BOOM.

The impact shook the desert floor. A wave of golden light exploded from the base of the stone, rippling outward in a perfect circle.

It passed through Vihaan. It passed through the jeep. It passed through the spirits.

Where the light touched the spirits, they didn’t fight. They didn’t scream. They simply vanished, dissolving into dust that settled instantly on the ground.

The wind stopped. The howling ceased. The shadows retreated.

Silence slammed back into the world. Heavy. Absolute.

Vihaan let go of the metal bar. It clattered to the ground. He fell to his knees, gasping for air, trembling uncontrollably. His body ached. his mind felt strangely light, filled with holes where memories used to be.

He looked up at the stone.

It stood perfectly upright. The symbols glowed softly, pulsing in a slow, steady rhythm. Like a heartbeat.

“It’s done,” he whispered. Tears blurred his vision. “It’s over.”

He closed his eyes, waiting for the sun to rise. Waiting for the road to open. Waiting for release.

But the silence stretched on.

And then, he felt it.

The familiar pull. The twist in his stomach. The folding of the sky.

Vihaan’s eyes snapped open in horror.

The world dissolved.

He stood beside the jeep.

The engine ticked. The desert was silent.

The stone stood before him.

The reset had happened again.

“No,” Vihaan breathed. “No, I fixed it! I fixed it!”

But as he looked closer, he saw that the stone was still upright. It hadn’t leaned back. It was fixed.

So why was he still here?

A new whisper drifted on the wind. Not evil. Not angry. Just… waiting.

“Balance is restored. But the debt is not paid.”
