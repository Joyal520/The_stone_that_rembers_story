CHAPTER 6

The City That Should Not Exist

Vihaan walked toward the city slowly.

He expected the desert to stop him. He expected the ground to pull him back or the sky to flicker and reset again. But nothing happened. Each step felt real. The sand shifted naturally under his feet. The air grew warmer as he moved closer to the light, as if the city carried its own weather.

Behind him, the leaning stone remained silent. Ahead of him, the city grew clearer.

At first, it looked like a mirage—shapes trembling in the heat, edges blurred, as if the desert tried to hide it. But with every step, the walls sharpened. Stone blocks rose out of the sand. Fires burned in metal bowls along wide paths. The sound of voices drifted through the air, calm and steady.

This was not a ruined city. It was alive.

Vihaan slowed at the edge, waiting for someone to stop him. No guards appeared. No warnings. No raised weapons. The city welcomed him with quiet stillness, like it had already decided he belonged there.

People walked along the paths. Men and women wore simple clothes made of rough fabric. Children sat near fires, drawing shapes in the sand. Old people rested against stone walls, watching the flames.

All of them looked human. Too human.

Their faces were familiar in a strange way—like people he had seen in dreams and forgotten the moment he woke. They moved calmly, but their calm felt practiced, like a mask they wore to hide something underneath.

Vihaan stepped inside.

The moment he crossed the invisible boundary, the fear in his chest loosened. His breathing slowed. His thoughts became clearer, lighter, almost peaceful.

And that frightened him.

Fear did not vanish like that. Not in the desert. It felt as if something had reached into his body and turned fear off like a switch.

He swallowed and forced his feet forward.

A young woman approached him carrying a clay cup filled with water. She smiled gently and held it out, as if she had been expecting him.

“You look tired,” she said.

Her voice was soft. Kind. Normal.

Vihaan hesitated, then took the cup. His methods shook as he drank. The water was cool and real. It washed the dust from his throat and eased the tightness in his chest.

“Thank you,” he said quietly.

The woman nodded and walked away without another word.

Vihaan stood there, confused. No one asked who he was. No one questioned why he had come. It was as if his arrival was not a surprise, but a scheduled event.

He walked deeper into the city. Fires crackled softly. The smell of cooked food filled the air. Somewhere, someone laughed—a sound so ordinary it almost hurt.

Yet something felt wrong.

The city had too many sounds and not enough life behind them.

When he looked closely, he noticed people repeating small actions. A man lifted a basket, set it down, then lifted it again as if he had forgotten he had already done it. A child drew the same circle in the sand, over and over, never changing the shape. A woman stirred a pot that held no steam.

They moved like memories looping.

A cold line of fear returned to Vihaan’s spine.

He glanced at the fires. The flames burned, but the light did not behave properly. It did not spread naturally. It stayed close to the fire bowls, as if the city refused to let darkness leave—or refused to let light escape.

Vihaan looked down at his feet. His shadow stretched behind him, clear and dark.

He looked up at the people around him.

They cast no shadows.

None.

His heart skipped. He stepped to the side, closer to a fire, trying to prove it to himself. The light touched their faces, their hands, their clothing—but no shadow formed on the ground.

It was as if the city held only their shapes, not their weight.

He backed away slowly. A child looked up at him and smiled.

Then the child’s eyes flicked past Vihaan—over his shoulder—toward the desert. The smile vanished instantly. The child lowered his head and began drawing again, faster now, pressing the stick into the sand as if he had been warned not to look.

Vihaan turned, but saw nothing behind him. Still, the hairs on his arms rose.

At the centre of the city, an old man sat near a large fire. His hair was long and white, tied back with a strip of leather. His face was lined deeply, but his eyes were sharp and calm.

When Vihaan looked at him, the old man raised his head. Their eyes met.

The old man smiled slightly, as if he had been waiting for this moment.

“You have come far,” the old man said.

Vihaan swallowed. “Where… where is this place?”

The old man did not answer directly. He gestured for Vihaan to sit. “You are safe here. For now.”

“For now?” Vihaan repeated.

The old man watched the fire for a moment before speaking again. “This city appears only when the desert is wounded,” he said.

Vihaan’s heart began to race again. “I didn’t mean to hurt anything. It was an accident.”

The old man nodded. “Most damage is.”

Vihaan felt a strange pressure in his chest. “Who are you?”

The old man lifted his eyes to the sky.

“We are the first keepers of this land. Long before roads. Long before engines. Long before your kind forgot how to listen.”

Vihaan shook his head slowly. “That’s not possible. This desert has been empty for centuries.”

The old man smiled again, but this time there was sadness in it. “Empty does not mean lifeless. It only means you stopped seeing.”

Vihaan remembered the spirits. The shadows. The screams. “What about the things outside? The ones in the sand.”

The old man’s expression darkened. “They are what came before balance. They fed on fear and chaos. They destroyed without purpose.”

“Evil spirits,” Vihaan whispered.

“Yes,” the old man said. “We feared them. That fear built the stones.”

Vihaan looked around the city again, unsettled. “If you built the stones, then why do you look like people?”

The old man’s gaze held him.

“We look like what you can accept,”

he said.

A chill moved through Vihaan. “What does that mean?”

The old man did not answer.

Vihaan’s fingers tightened. “I hit one of the stones. That’s why I’m trapped.”

The old man closed his eyes briefly. “Yes.”

Vihaan leaned forward. “So tell me how to fix it.”

The old man opened his eyes again. They seemed brighter now, almost glowing in the firelight. “First, you must understand what you broke.”

He stood slowly, using a carved staff for support. “Come. The night is not finished.”

Vihaan followed him through the city. People watched them pass, their faces calm but their eyes heavy, as if they carried a shared secret. As they walked deeper, the city became quieter. The fires burned lower. The warm smell of food faded into something drier.

Then Vihaan noticed something. The symbols. They were everywhere.

Carved into doorways. Etched into stones. Painted faintly on cloth. The same style as the leaning stone. The same sharp, desperate lines.

And some of them looked fresh. As if someone had carved them recently.

Vihaan stopped walking. “Why are these symbols here?”

The old man did not turn back immediately. “They are reminders.”

“Reminders of what?”

The old man finally looked at him. “Reminders of what happens when balance breaks.”

Vihaan’s throat tightened. “Are you trying to help me… or trap me?”

For the first time, the old man’s smile vanished. “Both can be true,” he said quietly.

They reached a wide stone platform at the far end of the city. From there, the desert stretched endlessly into darkness.

The old man pointed. “You broke a balance stone. It does not trap. It does not punish. It corrects.”

“Then why the loop?” Vihaan asked.

“Because imbalance repeats itself,” the old man said. “Until it is corrected.”

Vihaan clenched his fists. “So I just need to stand it upright?”

The old man turned to him sharply. “Careful. It is not that simple.”

The ground beneath them trembled slightly. Far away, a scream echoed across the desert. Not human.

The firelight flickered. Vihaan felt a familiar cold creeping into his chest.

The old man gripped his staff tightly. “They know you are here.”

“Who?” Vihaan asked.

The old man looked into the darkness. “The ones you freed.”

The city lights dimmed suddenly. The warmth began to fade. The people’s voices became quieter, thinner, like they were being pulled away.

Vihaan turned and stared back at the city. For a moment, he saw something he could not explain. One woman’s face flickered—just for a second—into something older, cracked, and empty, like a stone mask.

Then it returned to normal.

Vihaan stepped back, heart pounding. “What are you?” he whispered.

The old man did not answer. He only said, “Walk carefully, Vihaan.”

Then he added, almost too softly to hear:

“Because if the desert cannot correct you… it will replace you.”

And Vihaan realised something terrifying. The city was not permanent. It was already disappearing.

And the evil outside was getting closer.

But worse than that—He could no longer tell which side truly wanted him alive.
