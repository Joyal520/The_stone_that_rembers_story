CHAPTER 7

The Price of Balance

The city dimmed slowly, like a dying fire.

Vihaan felt the change in the air before he saw it. The warmth that had wrapped around him began to fade. The sounds of voices grew softer, thinner, as if they were being pulled away by something unseen. Fires still burned, but their light trembled, and the flames bent strangely, leaning toward the desert.

The old man stood very still beside him.

“They are coming closer,” the old man said.

Vihaan’s heart raced. “The evil spirits?”

“Yes,” the old man replied. “They feel the imbalance weakening.”

The ground trembled again, stronger this time. Dust rose from the stone platform and drifted into the air. Far away, in the darkness beyond the city, shapes moved. Vihaan could not see them clearly, but he felt them—watching, waiting.

The old man turned to him. “Listen carefully. There is little time.”

Vihaan nodded, forcing himself to stay calm.

“The stones were placed to hold the desert in balance,” the old man continued. “Each one was aligned carefully. Height, angle, position. Even a small change creates cracks in time.”

Vihaan remembered the crash. The sound of metal. The stone leaning more than before.

“I damaged it,” he said. “That’s why time keeps repeating.”

“Yes,” the old man said. “The desert resets because it is trying to correct itself. But it cannot fix what was broken by a human hand.”

Vihaan clenched his fists. “So I have to fix it.”

The old man nodded slowly. “But balance has a price.”

Vihaan looked at him. “What kind of price?”

Before the old man could answer, a sharp scream tore through the air. It came from the desert, closer now. The sound was wrong—too loud, too long, filled with anger.

The people of the city reacted immediately. They stopped what they were doing and turned toward the darkness. Their calm faces hardened. Some lowered their heads. Others closed their eyes, as if preparing for something painful.

Vihaan noticed something else.

Their bodies were becoming faint. Their edges blurred slightly, like smoke thinning in the wind.

“We cannot stay much longer,” the old man said. “The city exists only while balance is broken. When the stone is repaired, we will fade again.”

Vihaan swallowed. “Fade… as in disappear?”

The old man met his eyes. “As in return.”

Vihaan felt a strange pain in his chest. He had known these people for only a short time, yet the thought of losing them felt heavy.

“You said there was a price,” Vihaan said quietly. “What is it?”

The old man raised his staff and pointed toward the desert.

“To repair the stone,” he said, “you must do it during the reset.”

Vihaan frowned. “What does that mean?”

“You must act at the moment the desert repeats,” the old man said. “When time bends back on itself. That is when the stone is weakest.”

Vihaan’s mind raced. “But every time the reset happens, everything returns to how it was.”

“Yes,” the old man said. “Everything… except you.”

Vihaan felt cold. “Except me?”

“With every reset,” the old man continued, “a part of you is left behind. A memory. A feeling. A piece of who you are.”

Vihaan thought of the blurred days, the lost hours, the moments he could no longer remember clearly.

“That’s why I feel different,” he said. “That’s why time feels broken inside my head.”

The old man nodded. “The desert takes a price for correction.”

Vihaan looked toward the darkness where the screams came from. The evil spirits moved closer now. He could see their shapes clearly—tall, twisted, crawling along the sand like living shadows.

“If I fail,” Vihaan asked, “what happens?”

The old man did not answer immediately.

“If you fail,” he said finally, “the stone will fall completely. The locks will break. The desert will belong to them again.”

Vihaan’s stomach dropped. “And me?”

The old man’s voice softened.

“You will become part of the loop.”

Vihaan closed his eyes for a moment. Fear rushed through him, sharp and overwhelming. He wanted to run. He wanted to wake up. He wanted to believe none of this was real.

But he couldn’t.

He opened his eyes again. “Tell me how,” he said.

The old man placed a hand on Vihaan’s shoulder. It felt solid, warm—real.

“You must return to the stone,” he said. “You must lift it, align it, and seal the crack using what you have. Not tools alone. Intention matters.”

Vihaan nodded. “I’ll do it.”

The old man looked at him closely. “You will not remember everything after this,” he said. “Some of this will fade.”

Vihaan managed a weak smile. “As long as it ends.”

The old man returned the smile, though his eyes were sad. “Go now. The reset is near.”

The ground shook violently. The city lights flickered rapidly. Buildings blurred. Fires stretched into lines of light.

Vihaan felt the air pull away from him, dragging him backward.

“Wait!” he shouted. “Will I see you again?”

The old man’s voice echoed as the city dissolved.

“We are always here,” he said. “As long as balance is remembered.”

Vihaan stumbled. The world twisted. The sky folded inward. Time snapped back.

He stood beside the jeep again.

The stone loomed in front of him, leaning, cracked, waiting.

The desert screamed.

The evil spirits rushed toward him from every direction.

The reset had begun.

And Vihaan realised something terrifying—

This time, it might be his last chance.

Because the stone was already starting to fall.
