CHAPTER 9

The Road That Moved Forward

Vihaan did not open his eyes at first.

He lay still, listening. He waited for the wind to howl. He waited for the silence to press against his ears. He waited for the cold whisper of the spirits.

But he heard something else.

A bird. Singing.

He sat up slowly. He was in the jeep. The engine was off. The sun was rising—bright, golden, familiar. Not the pale, sickly light of the loop, but a true morning sun that warmed his skin.

He looked at the dashboard. Dust covered the gauges. The fuel needle was low, but not empty.

He looked out the window.

The stone was gone.

The road stretched ahead of him, straight and unbroken. No shadows twisted in the distance. No spirits watched from the dunes. Just sand, scrub brush, and the endless blue sky.

“It worked,” he whispered. His voice cracked, dry and unused.

He turned the key in the ignition. The engine coughed, sputtered, then roared to life. The sound was the most beautiful thing he had ever heard.

Vihaan shifted into gear and drove. He kept his eyes fixed on the odometer, watching the numbers tick upward.

One kilometre. Two. Five. Ten.

The road did not loop back. The landscape changed slowly, shifting from deep dunes to rocky plains.

An hour later, he saw buildings. A small roadside town. A gas station. A tea stall.

Vihaan pulled over, his hands shaking so hard he could barely turn off the engine. He stumbled out of the jeep and walked toward the stall. People sat on plastic chairs, drinking tea, talking, laughing.

Real people.

They cast shadows. Their voices carried weight. They looked at him with curiosity, not hunger.

Vihaan sat down heavily. An old man behind the counter looked at him.

“Water?” the man asked.

Vihaan nodded. He reached into his pocket and realised he had no money. His wallet was gone—left behind in the sand, or maybe it had never been there.

“I… I can’t pay,” Vihaan stammered.

The man paused, then placed a glass of water and a plate of biscuits on the table.

“Drink,” he said simply. “You look like you’ve been through hell, son.”

Vihaan drank the water in one gulp. It tasted like life.

“Thank you,” he whispered.

He caught his reflection in a small mirror hanging on the stall wall.

He froze.

The face looking back at him was his, but it was changed. His eyes were older, deeper. There were streaks of grey in his hair that hadn’t been there before. And on his hands…

He looked down at his palms.

Faint, white scars traced the lines of his skin. They looked like symbols. The same symbols that had been on the stone.

“The price,” he murmured.

He tried to remember the city. The faces of the people. The old man’s voice.

But they were slipping away. Like a dream fading in the morning light. He could remember the feeling of peace, the warmth of the fire, but the details were burying themselves in the sand of his memory.

“No,” he said. He grabbed a napkin from the table. “I have to remember.”

He searched his pockets and found a small stub of a pencil. He began to write. Furiously. Desperately.

He wrote about the loop. The stone. The spirits. The city. The balance.

The stall owner watched him concerned. “You okay there?”

Vihaan didn’t look up. “I just… I need to write it down. Before it goes.”

“Before what goes?”

Vihaan stopped. He looked at the words on the napkin. They looked crazy. Impossible.

But the scars on his hands were real.

“The truth,” Vihaan said softly.

He folded the napkin carefully and put it in his pocket. He looked out at the road, stretching away toward the horizon.

He was free.

But he knew he had left something behind in that desert. A part of himself that would always be standing there, beside the stone, making sure it didn’t fall.

“The debt is not paid,” the wind had said.

Vihaan stood up. “I know,” he whispered to the air. “I’ll remember. That is the payment.”

He walked back to the jeep.

And for the first time in a lifetime, he drove forward.
