CHAPTER 1

The Office Assignment

The office was quiet when the Vihaan received the task. Too quiet.

It was past six in the evening, the hour when most employees had already gone home. Only a few lights were still on, casting long shadows across the grey floor. The air felt heavy, as if the building itself was tired.

The Vihaan sat at his desk, checking the time again and again, wondering why he had been asked to stay late on his very first week.

A sudden knock broke the silence.

“Come in,” he said, trying to sound calm.

The manager stepped inside without a smile. He carried a thin brown package, sealed tightly with black tape. The manager placed it on the desk but did not let go immediately. For a moment, his hand rested on it, as if he was unsure whether to release it.

“This is your assignment,”

The Vihaan looked at the package. It felt heavier than paper should feel.

“What is it?” he asked.

“A document,” the manager replied. “Confidential.”

“Where do I deliver it?”

“Across the Pinnacle Desert. To the other side.”

The Vihaan frowned. He had heard the name before, but only in passing. No one spoke about the desert clearly. It was always mentioned quickly, then forgotten.

“Tonight?” the Vihaan asked.

“Yes. Before morning.

The Vihaan hesitated. “Sir… the Pinnacle Desert is dangerous, isn’t it?”

The manager looked away. His eyes moved to the window, then to the wall clock, anywhere but the Vihaan’s face.

“Just follow the route,” he said quietly. “Do not stop. Do not explore. And do not ask questions.”

That last sentence felt heavier than the package.

The Vihaan slowly took the document. The seal felt cold against his fingers. As soon as he touched it, the office lights flickered once, then returned to normal.

The Vihaan noticed it. The manager noticed it too.

Neither of them spoke.

“You will take the company jeep,” the manager said. “It is already prepared.”

“Has anyone done this route before?” the Vihaan asked.

The manager paused for a second too long.

“Yes,” he answered. “Others have.”

That was all he said.

The Vihaan stood up, holding the package tightly. “Sir… what happens if something goes wrong?”

The manager finally looked at him. His face was pale, his eyes tired.

“Nothing,” he said. “As long as you follow the route.”

The Vihaan nodded, though he did not feel convinced.

As he turned to leave, the manager added, “If you hear things… ignore them.”

The Vihaan stopped.

“Hear what, sir?”

But when he turned around, the manager had already left the room.

The office door closed softly behind him.

The Vihaan stood alone for a moment, listening. The building creaked. The air-conditioning hummed weakly.

Somewhere, a printer started printing, then stopped suddenly.

He walked out into the hallway. His footsteps echoed louder than they should have.

The lights above flickered again, this time longer. One by one, they dimmed, then returned.

As he walked, he felt something strange—like someone was walking just behind him, matching his steps exactly.

He stopped. The footsteps stopped.

His heart beat faster. Slowly, he turned around.

The hallway was empty.

No one stood there. No movement. Just long shadows stretching along the floor.

He told himself it was only his imagination.

When he reached the exit, the automatic doors opened with a low mechanical sound. A cold wind brushed his face, even though it was supposed to be a warm evening.

Outside, the sky looked wrong.

Dark clouds moved quickly, too quickly, covering the moon. The streetlights flickered, then steadied.

The parking lot was almost empty, except for one jeep parked near the gate.

The company jeep.

As the Vihaan walked toward it, he heard a faint whisper.

At first, he thought it was the wind.

Then he realised the wind was not blowing.

The whisper sounded like his name.

The Vihaan stopped walking.

“Hello?” he called softly.

No answer.

The whisper came again, closer this time, but he could not understand the words. It sounded old, broken, and angry.

He opened the jeep door quickly and got inside. The moment he closed the door, the whisper stopped.

His hands shook as he placed the package on the seat beside him.

He started the engine. The headlights turned on, shining into the darkness ahead.

In the rear-view mirror, for just a second, he thought he saw a shadow standing behind the jeep.

He blinked.

The shadow was gone.

He drove out of the office compound without looking back.

As the jeep moved forward, the office building behind him went completely dark, as if it had never been occupied at all.

Above him, the clouds closed tightly over the sky.

And far away, beyond the city lights, the Pinnacle Desert waited.

Something had already noticed him.
