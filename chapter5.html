CHAPTER 5

The Weight of Repetition

Vihaan did not know how many times the desert reset.

At first, he tried to count. After the second reset, he scratched lines into the sand near the jeep, marking each loop carefully. After the third, he used a sharp piece of broken metal from the jeep to cut a small mark into the leaning stone. He told himself that if even one mark remained, he would know he was not losing his mind.

But when the desert reset again, the scratches were gone. The cut had disappeared.

Only the stone remained—still leaning, still wrong.

Time erased everything else.

The desert returned to the same moment again and again, as if nothing had happened. The same sky. The same shadows. The same silence. Even the pain in his body faded with each reset, leaving only the memory of it behind.

Vihaan sat beside the jeep and waited, hoping something would change on its own.

Nothing did.

The sun crept slowly across the sky, then jumped back to its earlier position without warning. Shadows stretched long across the sand, then snapped back like broken rubber bands. Each reset felt identical at first.

Then they began to change.

On the fourth reset, Vihaan heard breathing behind him while he stood alone. He turned quickly, heart racing, but saw nothing. The sound faded, as if it had never existed.

On the fifth reset, he saw footprints in the sand approaching him—his own boots, walking toward him from the darkness. He backed away, shaking, until the footprints stopped inches from his feet.

On the sixth reset, he heard his name spoken clearly.

“Vihaan.”

The voice sounded exactly like his own. He covered his ears, but the sound came from inside his head.

“Stop,” he whispered. “Please.”

The desert did not answer.

With each repetition, the evil spirits grew bolder.

They no longer hid at the edges of his vision. They stood openly in the distance, their tall, thin shapes clear against the dark sand. Their hollow eyes followed him wherever he moved. Sometimes they stepped closer. Sometimes they simply waited, as if they knew time itself was on their side.

They fed on the repetition.

Vihaan tried to leave again.

He climbed into the jeep and started driving the moment the reset released him. He pushed the engine hard, faster than before, forcing the vehicle across the desert road. The darkness rushed past him. The headlights shook.

The road stretched endlessly ahead.

Then the engine slowed.

The jeep stalled on its own, stopping perfectly.

In front of him stood the stone. Again.

The engine died. The silence returned.

Vihaan screamed and slammed his fists against the steering wheel.

“No!” he shouted. “Let me go!”

A laugh rolled across the desert. It did not come from one place. It came from everywhere.

Vihaan stepped out of the jeep. The air felt heavier now, thick and dry, as if each breath had to be pulled from the desert by force. The symbols on the stone looked deeper than before, sharper, as if they were being carved again and again by unseen hands.

Without thinking, he reached out and touched the stone.

Pain exploded through his head. He fell to his knees as images flooded his mind.

He saw ancient people standing in the desert long before roads existed. They wore simple clothes and carried tools made of stone and bone. He felt their fear as shadows crawled across the land, destroying everything they touched.

He saw those people working together, raising tall stones across the desert. Many stones. Not just one. They placed them carefully, measuring angles, distance, balance. Each stone glowed faintly as it was set upright.

He understood without words. The stones were not decorations. They were locks.

He pulled his hand away with a cry, gasping for breath.

The spirits screamed in anger.

“YOU REMEMBER NOW.”

Vihaan staggered backward.

The next reset came faster. So did the one after that.

Time stopped behaving in ways he could understand. Sometimes the sun reset before it fully set. Sometimes the night lasted only seconds. His body grew tired, but sleep never felt real. Each time he closed his eyes, he woke up standing beside the jeep again.

His memories began to blur.

He forgot how many loops had passed. He forgot how long he had been trapped. At times, he forgot why he was afraid—until the spirits reminded him.

They showed him visions.

In one loop, he saw himself lying dead in the sand, eyes open, mouth filled with dust.

In another, he saw the leaning stone fall completely, breaking into pieces. Shadows poured across the desert, covering everything.

In another, he saw a city burning, swallowed by darkness.

“You cannot fix it,” the spirits whispered. “You are too late.”

Vihaan collapsed beside the stone, shaking.

“I didn’t mean to break it,” he said. “I didn’t know.

One spirit stepped closer than the others. Its shape flickered, unstable, like a flame fighting strong wind.

“INTENT DOES NOT MATTER,”

it said.

“ONLY BALANCE.”

Vihaan laughed weakly. “Then tell me how to fix it.”

The spirit smiled.

It was the worst thing he had seen so far.

“You must remember.”

As the knowledge settled into him, the desert responded.

The ground trembled lightly. The sky darkened. The air shifted.

Then, far away, something changed. Vihaan noticed it slowly, like a dream forming.

A faint glow appeared on the horizon. Warm. Steady. Different from the cold light of the spirits.

He stood up slowly, heart pounding.

Lights. A city.

It had not been there before.

Tall shapes formed where the glow was strongest. Walls. Buildings. Fires. The sound of distant voices carried softly across the sand.

Hope rose inside him before he could stop it.

The spirits reacted instantly. They shrieked in rage. Their forms twisted and thinned, pulling away from the light.

“NO. NOT YET.”

Vihaan stared at the city. “What is that place?” he asked.

The spirits backed away, retreating into the sand like smoke pulled downward.

“The balance remembers. And it will judge you.”

The desert fell silent again. Vihaan stood alone, staring at the distant city. The light did not flicker. It did not fade.

It waited.

For the first time since the loops began, the stone did not pull him back immediately. The ground beneath his feet felt open.

Vihaan took a step toward the city. Nothing stopped him.

He took another step. Still nothing.

Behind him, the stone leaned in silence, its symbols glowing faintly. Ahead of him, the city waited.

And for the first time, the desert did not reset.
